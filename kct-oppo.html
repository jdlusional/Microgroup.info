Opposition-research profile — Democratic candidate, Tennessee's 9th Congressional District (2026 primary). State Representative, House District 86 (Memphis).

Compiled July 2026 · Every item tagged for verification status, quality, and source

Executive summary

This is a thin file, and honesty about that is the value. Across roughly thirty research workstreams and a full adversarial-verification pass, the genuinely clean, fair, hard-to-rebut material that is directly about Pearson's own conduct reduces to one marquee item — his non-participation in the decisive April 22, 2026 vote to take over his own district's schools — supported by a defensible "attention, not delivery" narrative. Most of the rest is guilt-by-association (real but indirect), context-dependent (loses on a fact-check if clipped), or actively backfire-prone.

Pearson has a short, ideologically consistent record with no criminal or financial-scandal history, a compelling personal brand, and a martyrdom narrative (his 2023 expulsion) that punishes clumsy attacks. The professional play is to build on the few clean facts and discard the rest — reaching is how opposition research backfires.

The five biggest opposition points

1. The April 22, 2026 school-takeover non-vote★★★★★

When the legislature cast the deciding vote to strip local control of Memphis-Shelby County Schools — his own district — Pearson was present and voting earlier that day, then was not recorded on the takeover vote and was not on the excused list, while every other Memphis Democrat stayed and voted no. The one clean, primary-sourced, directly-about-him, fact-check-proof hit in the file. This is the lead.

Frame precisely: "present earlier, then not recorded on the vote, not excused" — never "absent all day" (refutable). Do not speculate about where he was.
2. "Attention, not delivery"★★★★☆

No bill he prime-sponsored ever became substantive state law (his signature gun bill died without a seconding motion); ~65% of his itemized money comes from out of state; a national-media brand that dwarfs his legislative output.

Only if framed precisely: never "65% of his money" (it's 65% of itemized, ~36% of total); always concede the GOP supermajority context, or the "no laws" point collapses.
3. Environmental brand vs. results★★★☆☆

His marquee current fight (xAI's Boxtown gas turbines) has produced no win, and the Sierra Club — which gave him its top environmental award — admitted taking $26M from a fracking company. Undercuts his central credential.

The xAI fight has a David-vs-Goliath sympathy risk (a Trump-DOJ intervention) — attack the lack of result, not the cause. Keep the Sierra Club $26M gas money separate from its unrelated $28K FEC fine.
4. Hard votes for an R+21 district★★★☆☆

Voted against expanding the death penalty, against the bail-denial amendment, and said "abolish ICE" on camera. Genuinely weaponizable in a Trump+21 seat.

General election only, and only with honest framing — the dishonest glosses ("sides with child rapists") backfire. None of this works in the Democratic primary.
5. Ideological maximalism★★☆☆☆

Verbatim 2023 quotes: "we need… a new country"; the tools of "exploitative capitalism" won't "dismantle the house… brick by brick." Vivid and confirmed.

Handle with care: each is a civil-rights-renewal metaphor ("the house" = institutions) that loses a fact-check if clipped. Usable only as a "moderate 2026 packaging vs. maximalist worldview" contrast, quoted in full.

Supporting material (Tier 2)

  • Byhalia origin story — overstated. He was a genuine co-founder and public face (one of three), but "he beat the pipeline" overstates a shared, overdetermined win: the company cited COVID oil economics, no court ever ruled against it, and binding local law passed after cancellation. No credit-theft grievance exists — don't run that angle.
  • Equation Campaign (transparency/optics only). He sits on the advisory board of a Rockefeller-funded grantmaker that gave his co-founded nonprofit $165K. Not self-dealing, not a governing-board seat — calling him a grantmaker "director" is refutable with the org's own IRS filing.
  • Financial-disclosure anomaly. His U.S. House disclosure reports a salaried state office but lists no earned income on Schedule C, which appears incomplete. Frame as accuracy of his own sworn filing, not "hidden income."

How this file was built

This profile was produced by a structured, multi-agent deep-research process designed to be fact-check-hardened, not merely comprehensive.

  • Parallel deep research. Roughly forty independent research agents ran across about thirty workstreams (attendance, legislative productivity, campaign finance, nonprofit/ethics, rhetoric, public safety, environment, endorsements, associations, social media, long-form interviews), throttled to a standing cap of ten concurrent.
  • Primary-source-first. Claims were anchored wherever possible to primary records — Tennessee House Journals and roll calls, raw FEC filings, the U.S. House financial disclosure, IRS 990s, and the Center for Effective Lawmaking — over news or aggregators.
  • Explicit tagging. Every item carries a verification tag (Confirmed / Unverified / Gap / Misattributed / Do-not-use) and a quality rating, so the evidentiary weight is always visible.
  • Adversarial re-verification. Each marquee claim was independently re-checked by a separate agent instructed to refute it. The attendance lead and the finance spine were triple-verified; quotes, votes, ethics, and associations were double-verified.
  • Errors caught and corrected included a misattributed quote, a campaign-finance geography artifact caught twice independently, four nonexistent or misattributed awards, an overstatement of an advisory board seat as a governing one, and a nonprofit title that belongs to a relative.
  • Walled-source discipline. Sources automated tools could not reach (verbatim video, IRS Tax-Exempt-Organization-Search, state campaign-finance portals, JS-gated filings) were routed to a human pull list rather than guessed.
