Custodian

Custodian now works in three layers. History: who each foundation's named investment manager is, from their own Form 990 filings, and when that relationship last changed. Conditions: a readiness score built from evidence-backed variables (performance, tenure, structural lock-in) that separates foundations likely to shop from foundations that merely could. Events: a live monitor watching trade press, RFP postings, and foundation pages for the moment a search actually surfaces. This page remains a no-charge prototype for Calvin, per what was already discussed.

Scope, as given: Central Texas foundations reporting $5M+ in investments and/or interest-bearing cash. 223 organizations cleared that bar in the full cached filing corpus: 11 name an identifiable investment manager, 191 show no named manager at all, and every figure traces to a public IRS filing. Nothing here is estimated or invented.

Layer 3: live detection

The Signals Monitor

A daily monitor is built, soaked, and now live in alerting mode: its shakedown window ran clean (five runs, zero noise candidates), so verified findings now surface the day they land. It watches compliant, verified sources only: trade-press feeds that publish institutional searches and hires by name, foundation and association pages where investment RFPs actually get posted, and provider-acquisition news, which industry data says is the single strongest non-performance trigger for a manager review. Only substantive signal classes alert; a source that cannot be checked is recorded honestly, never emailed daily. When a watched source names a search, the finding carries the URL, timestamp, and exact text, never an inference.

The cards above are labeled format specimens, not detections: the monitor is in its shakedown window and no verified finding has been promoted to this page yet. When one is, it will replace a specimen in this same format.

Method: what this is, and what it isn't

Three layers, each labeled with its evidence

Where this comes from. Every row traces to public IRS filings: Part VII Section B contractor disclosures (990) or Part VIII (990-PF) for named managers; Part VII Section A officer and trustee lists for decision-makers; Part IX investment-management fee lines, Part X / Part II balance sheets, and investment income lines for the scoring variables. The monitor adds verified public web sources: trade-press RSS feeds whose terms permit polling, foundation and association pages fetched once daily with ordinary politeness. Sources whose terms prohibit automated access (one major trade publication, LinkedIn, job boards) are deliberately not scraped, stated here plainly.

How the readiness score earns its numbers. Each variable carries an evidence tier, and the tiers moved as the data deepened: after a 1,537-filing history backfill brought every foundation's full available filing record into the corpus, the local backtest's early 2.7x underperformance signal did not hold (the flag now fires on too few filing-pairs to validate or refute anything locally). So the honest statement is this: sustained underperformance versus size peers is weighted on the published research (Goyal-Wahal 2008 and successors, using a 990-derived return method with published precedent), with our own backtest inconclusive at current sample size, and this page will say so until the sample grows. Verified by industry data, not yet observable in our window: provider mergers and acquisitions (survey data says ownership change at the incumbent is the leading non-performance trigger; the monitor watches for these events). Tested and found wanting, so zero-weighted: a fee-ratio outlier variable and a leadership-turnover variable showed no predictive lift; they are computed and shown, but count for nothing until better evidence arrives. Inertia markers (bank-trust arrangements, extreme cash allocations) actually predicted FEWER switches, so they appear as opportunity context, never as switch propensity. Structural lock-in dampens everything multiplicatively: the clearest case, a foundation whose "manager" is the founder's own family office, is scored as very unlikely to shop no matter what else fires.

What "tenure" and "cadence read" mean. Unchanged from the first pass: a "recent change" requires a verified change of firm, not paperwork variance. Two verified non-changes are excluded by name (a 2021 industry rebrand; a 2022 family-office restructuring), and a parsing artifact that once fabricated two false changes was found and fixed by reading raw filings. Fiduciary best practice treats 3-5 years as a normal re-bid cycle, which is what the tenure-overdue flag measures against.

The honest limits. Form 990 filings lag 10-18 months, so filing-derived facts are conditions, not events; only the monitor layer is fresh. Contractor disclosure has two blind spots: the $100K compensation threshold, and the five-highest-paid cap, which can crowd an investment manager off the list of a large operating organization (at least one billion-dollar foundation in the whitespace segment reports investment-management fees with no manager named, almost certainly this effect). Decision-maker rosters carry their filing year; boards change between filings. A foundation absent from these lists may still have a manager this method cannot see. And a readiness score is a screen, not a verdict: it ranks where attention is likely to pay off, it does not predict any single foundation's behavior.
